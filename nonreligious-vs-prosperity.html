Why "non-religiousness" and not "secularism"?

Secularism is a political concept — the separation of religion from state institutions. India is the clearest example of why this distinction matters: it is constitutionally secular, yet 93% of its population is deeply religious in daily social life. It ranks 134th on HDI. This study measures social non-religiousness — the degree to which people actually live free of religious doctrine, superstition, and faith-based decision-making, regardless of what their constitution says.

Why 150 countries and not 195?

The remaining ~40 countries with no data are micro-states (San Marino, Liechtenstein), active conflict zones with no credible polling (Somalia, South Sudan), or closed authoritarian regimes (Turkmenistan, North Korea has estimates only). No country was excluded to improve the correlation.

Data sources — three independent, cross-referenced

  • Pew Research Center — Global Religious Futures 2010–2020 (230 countries and territories). Gold standard for methodology and sample size.
  • Gallup International / WIN — Annual polls, 2024–25 wave covering 42–68 countries. Self-reported: "religious / not religious / convinced atheist."
  • World Values Survey Wave 7 — 2017–2022, 90 countries, face-to-face representative samples. Measures belief in God directly.
  • GDP per capita — World Bank / IMF, nominal USD 2023–24.
  • HDI — UNDP Human Development Report 2023–24. Composite: income + education + life expectancy.

The statistical correlation

Across all 150 countries, the Pearson correlation between non-religiousness % and GDP per capita is approximately r ≈ 0.61. Against HDI — a better metric since it includes education and life expectancy — it is stronger: r ≈ 0.68. Both are statistically significant at p < 0.001.

This is consistent with peer-reviewed findings: Ruck et al. (2018, Science Advances) found that secularization preceded GDP growth across the 20th century — causation, not just correlation. Norris & Inglehart (2011) in "Sacred and Secular" similarly found that existential security drives secularization, which in turn enables better institutions.

The honest outliers — and why they strengthen the argument

  • Gulf oil states (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait): High GDP from geological accident, not institutional modernization. Their HDI significantly underperforms secular peers at equivalent income levels. Women's rights and political freedoms remain severely limited.
  • Vietnam: High non-religiousness (~63%) from communist legacy, not organic development. However, Vietnam's GDP has doubled since 2015 — its trajectory is the fastest-rising in Asia.
  • United States: The most striking outlier among wealthy nations. Only ~30% non-religious yet highest GDP. However, its HDI, healthcare outcomes, inequality (Gini), and social mobility all significantly lag behind its secular wealthy peers (Norway, Denmark, Sweden, Australia).
